\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mental health is increasingly recognised as a critical component of overall well-being,
yet the tools used to assess it in digital health applications have seen limited
innovation. Most existing health-tracking applications rely on fixed, repetitive
questionnaires — typically presented as weekly or monthly check-ins — that ask the same
set of questions regardless of a user's current condition or history
\citep{aihw2012, malanin2025}.
This static approach fails to capture how a person's mental state evolves over time,
often producing assessments that feel irrelevant or burdensome and recommendations that
may be inaccurate or potentially harmful \citep{wang2023}.

The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) offers a new opportunity to address this
limitation. Unlike fixed rule-based branching logic, LLMs can dynamically generate
context-aware questions informed by a user's individual health profile, prior responses,
and longitudinal history \citep{tamquest2024, parkrobot2025}.
This enables a more personalised and human-centric assessment experience, where questions
adapt to what matters most for each individual at each point in time.

To evaluate this opportunity, we developed \emph{Navigator}, a web-based prototype that
implements two distinct monthly mental health check-in methods within the same
application environment:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rule-based method:} predefined branching logic determines which
        follow-up questions appear based on fixed risk-level thresholds.
  \item \textbf{LLM-based method:} Google Gemini 2.5 Flash dynamically generates
        personalised questions from the user's health profile and prior assessment data.
\end{itemize}
Using a within-subjects study design, participants interacted with both methods and
completed a structured feedback survey evaluating each approach across three dimensions:
\emph{Clarity}, \emph{Quality}, and \emph{Relevance}.

This study aims to answer the following research questions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Clarity):} Do LLM-generated questionnaires produce higher perceived
        clarity than rule-based questionnaires?
  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Quality):} Do LLM-generated questionnaires produce higher perceived
        quality than rule-based questionnaires?
  \item \textbf{RQ3 (Relevance):} Do LLM-generated questionnaires produce higher perceived
        relevance than rule-based questionnaires?
\end{itemize}

The main contributions of this work are:
(1) the Navigator prototype — a Flutter web application implementing both methods within
a unified interface;
(2) a within-subjects user study design using a standardised persona (Sophie) to ensure
fair comparison across conditions; and
(3) an empirical evaluation of user perceptions of Clarity, Quality, and Relevance for
LLM-based versus rule-based adaptive mental health questionnaires.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on mental health questionnaire systems,
LLMs in healthcare, and adaptive survey design.
Section~\ref{sec:system} describes the system architecture and both methods.
Section~\ref{sec:study} details the study design and evaluation metrics.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results, followed by discussion in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}, and conclusions in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.
